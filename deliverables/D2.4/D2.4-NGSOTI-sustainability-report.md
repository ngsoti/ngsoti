# Executive Summary

The NGSOTI project has supported the development, improvement, deployment, and operational use of several open-source tools that contribute directly to the cybersecurity capabilities of CIRCL and its constituency. These tools include, among others, **Rulezet**, **Kunai**, and **Vulnerability-Lookup**, including its related work around **GCVE**.

The sustainability of these tools after the end of the project is essential. They are not standalone research outputs, but operational assets that support real-world cybersecurity missions, including vulnerability disclosure, incident response, detection engineering, threat intelligence sharing, training, and coordination with national and international communities.

CIRCL intends to ensure the continuity of these tools through a mixed sustainability model combining:

- Integration into CIRCL’s core operational activities;
- Alignment with national responsibilities under the **NIS 2 Directive**;
- Continued use in **Coordinated Vulnerability Disclosure (CVD)** activities;
- Support for the **GCVE** initiative as part of decentralized vulnerability identification and disclosure;
- Limited private funding through professional services, especially for Vulnerability-Lookup;
- Reuse of the tools in standard CIRCL training activities;
- Extension of the software through new national, European, defence, or law-enforcement-related programmes;
- Continued publication and maintenance as open-source software.

This sustainability model aims to avoid dependence on a single funding source. Instead, it establishes a pragmatic and diversified approach where each tool is maintained according to its operational relevance, community adoption, and strategic value.

# Context and Objectives

NGSOTI has contributed to the development and support of open-source software that strengthens cybersecurity preparedness, detection, vulnerability management, and information sharing. These tools are aligned with CIRCL’s long-standing strategy of building reusable, transparent, and community-driven software for the cybersecurity ecosystem.

The objective of this sustainability plan is to describe how the software developed or supported during NGSOTI will remain maintained, relevant, and usable after the formal end of the project.

The sustainability plan addresses the following objectives:

1. **Ensure operational continuity**  
   The tools must remain usable by CIRCL, national stakeholders, and relevant communities after the end of the project.

2. **Maintain open-source availability**  
   The software should remain publicly available under open-source licences, allowing reuse, inspection, contribution, and deployment by third parties.

3. **Integrate tools into CIRCL’s regular missions**  
   Where relevant, the tools will become part of CIRCL’s standard operational, training, coordination, and support activities.

4. **Support national and European cybersecurity obligations**  
   The tools will contribute to activities related to the NIS 2 Directive, Coordinated Vulnerability Disclosure, vulnerability coordination, and cyber threat intelligence.

5. **Diversify maintenance and funding models**  
   Sustainability will rely on a combination of institutional support, project-based development, professional services, training activities, and potential cooperation with other public-sector programmes.

6. **Enable further development through new projects**  
   CIRCL will seek opportunities to extend these tools with new features and integrations through follow-up projects and complementary funding schemes.

# Scope of the Sustainability Plan

This plan focuses on the open-source software developed, improved, or supported by CIRCL during NGSOTI, including:

- **Rulezet**
- **Kunai**
- **Vulnerability-Lookup**
- **GCVE-related components and processes**

The sustainability model also applies more broadly to the surrounding ecosystem of documentation, training materials, deployment practices, community engagement, and operational know-how developed during the project.

# Sustainability Principles

The sustainability approach is based on several guiding principles.

## Open Source by Default

The software will remain open source wherever possible. This is essential for transparency, auditability, reproducibility, and community adoption.

Open-source availability also allows external users, researchers, public-sector organisations, CSIRTs, CERTs, companies, and academic partners to deploy the tools independently and contribute improvements.

## Operational Relevance

Sustainability will focus on tools that have a clear operational role. Software that is actively used in CIRCL’s daily missions or by its constituency will receive priority for maintenance, documentation, and integration.

## Community Reuse

The long-term value of the software depends on its adoption beyond the original project consortium. CIRCL will continue to encourage reuse through public repositories, documentation, examples, training, and engagement with relevant communities.

## Multiple Funding Sources

No single funding source should be considered sufficient to guarantee sustainability. CIRCL will rely on a mixed model combining:

- CIRCL’s national mission and funding;
- Activities linked to NIS 2;
- CVD and national coordination responsibilities;
- Professional services for selected tools;
- New EU and national projects;
- Training activities;
- Potential defence or law-enforcement cooperation programmes.

## Minimal but Reliable Maintenance

For some tools, sustainability does not necessarily mean continuous large-scale development. It may involve ensuring that the software remains installable, secure, documented, and compatible with current environments.

The goal is to maintain a realistic balance between ambition and available resources.


# Sustainability Model by Tool

## Vulnerability-Lookup

### Role and Strategic Importance

Vulnerability-Lookup is a key software component for the collection, correlation, publication, and analysis of vulnerability information. It supports operational vulnerability management, vulnerability intelligence, and coordination activities.

During and after NGSOTI, Vulnerability-Lookup is expected to remain a strategic tool for CIRCL, especially in relation to:

- Vulnerability disclosure;
- Coordinated Vulnerability Disclosure;
- National vulnerability coordination activities;
- Enrichment of vulnerability information;
- Support for GCVE identifiers and related metadata;
- Public and private-sector vulnerability intelligence workflows;
- Integration with existing cybersecurity tools and feeds.

### Sustainability through CIRCL Operations

Vulnerability-Lookup will remain part of CIRCL’s operational tooling. Its maintenance is aligned with CIRCL’s role as a national cybersecurity actor and its activities related to vulnerability management and information sharing.

CIRCL will continue to use Vulnerability-Lookup to support vulnerability analysis and coordination. This operational dependency creates a strong incentive to keep the software maintained, secure, and usable.

### Sustainability through Professional Services

A limited part of the sustainability of Vulnerability-Lookup can be supported through private funding via professional services.

CIRCL has established a professional services offering around Vulnerability-Lookup:

[Vulnerability lookup professional services](https://www.vulnerability-lookup.org/professional-services/)


These services may include, depending on needs and available resources:

- Deployment support;
- Custom integration;
- Data-feed integration;
- Operational support;
- Training;
- Feature development;
- Advisory services;
- Support for organisations adopting Vulnerability-Lookup.

This professional services model is not intended to turn the project into a closed commercial product. Rather, it provides a complementary funding stream that can support maintenance and targeted improvements while keeping the core software open source.

### Sustainability through New Projects

CIRCL plans to use future projects to extend Vulnerability-Lookup with new features. Possible areas of development include:

- Improved vulnerability enrichment;
- Enhanced API capabilities;
- Better support for decentralized vulnerability publication;
- Integration with additional vulnerability data sources;
- Improved user interfaces;
- Advanced search and correlation features;
- Stronger support for CSIRT and CERT workflows;
- Additional automation for vulnerability triage;
- Better support for machine-readable vulnerability information;
- Integration with training and simulation environments.

This approach allows the tool to evolve according to operational needs while ensuring that improvements funded through future projects remain available to the wider community.


## GCVE

### Role and Strategic Importance

GCVE is an initiative designed to support decentralized vulnerability identification and publication. It introduces a model where GCVE Numbering Authorities can allocate identifiers in a more flexible and distributed manner.

The GCVE work is closely related to Vulnerability-Lookup and CIRCL’s broader activities in vulnerability disclosure and vulnerability coordination.

GCVE is strategically important because it supports:

- Decentralized vulnerability publication;
- Increased resilience in vulnerability identification;
- Support for communities or organisations that need identifier allocation capabilities;
- Better alignment with open-source and community-based disclosure processes;
- Improved support for vulnerability coordination outside strictly centralized models.

### Sustainability through CVD Activities

CIRCL’s activities as a national coordinator for Coordinated Vulnerability Disclosure provide a natural sustainability path for GCVE-related work.

CVD requires reliable processes, identifiers, coordination workflows, and publication mechanisms. GCVE can contribute to this by offering a practical and open approach to vulnerability identification and referencing.

As part of its CVD mission, CIRCL can continue to maintain and refine GCVE-related tooling, documentation, and processes.

### Sustainability through NIS 2-Related Activities

The NIS 2 Directive increases the importance of structured vulnerability handling, incident reporting, coordination, and support to essential and important entities. GCVE can support these activities by contributing to the broader ecosystem of vulnerability coordination and disclosure.

CIRCL expects that some GCVE-related activities will be maintained as part of its responsibilities under NIS 2 and its role in supporting the national cybersecurity community.

### Sustainability through Vulnerability-Lookup Integration

GCVE is expected to remain closely integrated with Vulnerability-Lookup. This integration provides a practical operational home for GCVE data, APIs, synchronization mechanisms, and publication workflows.

The sustainability of GCVE is therefore linked to the sustainability of Vulnerability-Lookup. Improvements to Vulnerability-Lookup can also strengthen GCVE adoption and usability.

## Rulezet

### Role and Strategic Importance

Rulezet supports the management, publication, and use of detection rules and related cybersecurity knowledge. It contributes to detection engineering, operational security monitoring, and knowledge sharing.

Rulezet is relevant for CIRCL’s activities because detection rules are a practical bridge between threat intelligence, incident response, and operational defence.

### Sustainability through CIRCL’s Core Activities

For Rulezet, the primary sustainability path is integration into CIRCL’s standard operational activities. The tool can support CIRCL’s mission under the NIS 2 Directive by helping maintain and distribute detection-related knowledge to the constituency.

Rulezet can be used to support:

- Detection engineering;
- Sharing of rule sets;
- Training on detection logic;
- Support to constituents improving their monitoring capabilities;
- Documentation of detection approaches;
- Operational collaboration with trusted communities.

### Sustainability through NIS 2

The NIS 2 Directive strengthens the need for improved cybersecurity capabilities among essential and important entities. Detection and monitoring are key elements of this effort.

Rulezet can contribute to this objective by providing a reusable open-source platform for managing detection rules and helping organisations improve their operational detection capabilities.

As a result, Rulezet maintenance can be justified as part of CIRCL’s wider national role in supporting cybersecurity preparedness and resilience.

### Sustainability through Training

Rulezet can also be included in CIRCL’s standard training activities. Training creates a practical reason to maintain the software, documentation, and examples.

By using Rulezet in training sessions, CIRCL can ensure that:

- The tool remains understandable and usable;
- Documentation remains up to date;
- Exercises reflect real operational needs;
- Feedback from participants is used to improve the software;
- The tool reaches new users and communities.

Training activities supported under national funding can therefore contribute indirectly to Rulezet sustainability.

## Kunai

### Role and Strategic Importance

Kunai is an open-source tool that supports endpoint visibility, detection, and operational security use cases. It can contribute to incident response, threat hunting, telemetry collection, and security monitoring.

Its value lies in providing practical capabilities that can be reused in operational environments, exercises, and training.

### Sustainability through Operational Use

Kunai can be sustained through continued use in CIRCL’s operational and training activities. If the tool remains useful for incident response, telemetry collection, or detection engineering, it will remain part of CIRCL’s maintained tooling portfolio.

Operational use is one of the strongest sustainability mechanisms because it ensures that the software is tested against real requirements and receives feedback from practitioners.

### Sustainability through Training

Kunai can be included in CIRCL’s standard training programme, especially in areas such as:

- Endpoint visibility;
- Incident response;
- Threat hunting;
- Detection engineering;
- Security monitoring;
- Practical exercises for CSIRT and SOC teams.

Training usage provides a concrete incentive to maintain examples, deployment instructions, documentation, and compatibility with current systems.

### Sustainability through Additional Programmes

Kunai may also be relevant for future programmes involving defence, law enforcement, or other public-sector stakeholders. Such programmes may require tools for endpoint monitoring, forensic support, incident response, or controlled training environments.

Where appropriate and compatible with CIRCL’s mission, Kunai could be extended through such programmes while keeping the core open-source model.

This includes:

- Maintaining public source-code repositories;
- Reviewing issues and pull requests when resources permit;
- Publishing releases when meaningful changes are available;
- Keeping installation and deployment documentation up to date;

# Risks and Mitigation Measures

## Risk: Insufficient Maintenance Resources

There is a risk that available resources will not be sufficient to maintain all tools at the same level.

### Mitigation

CIRCL will prioritise maintenance based on operational relevance, community adoption, and strategic importance. A minimal maintenance model will be applied where full active development is not possible.

## Risk: Dependency on Project-Based Funding

Project-based funding is useful for development but may not ensure long-term maintenance.

### Mitigation

CIRCL will combine project funding with operational use, national funding, training, professional services, and potential public-sector programmes.

## Risk: Limited External Adoption

Some tools may remain useful internally but receive limited external adoption.

### Mitigation

CIRCL will improve documentation, provide training, present the tools in relevant communities, and encourage reuse through practical examples.

## Risk: Technical Debt

Open-source tools developed under project constraints may accumulate technical debt.

### Mitigation

CIRCL will address technical debt progressively, especially when tools are reused in operations, training, or future projects.

## Risk: Sustainability of Dependencies

The tools may depend on external libraries, data sources, or platforms that change over time.

### Mitigation

CIRCL will monitor critical dependencies and update software where needed. Future projects may also support refactoring or modernization.

## API Documentation Maintenance and Reference Approach

The technical interfaces described in this deliverable concern software components and services whose APIs evolve continuously over the lifetime of the project. This applies in particular to Rulezet, Kunai, Vulnerability-Lookup, and the GCVE-related components and processes.

As these tools are actively developed, their APIs may be extended, refined, reorganised, or otherwise updated in response to implementation progress, integration requirements, security improvements, user feedback, and operational needs. Consequently, a static description of all API endpoints, parameters, data models, and response structures within a formal EU deliverable would risk becoming outdated shortly after submission.

To ensure that beneficiaries, reviewers, implementers, and external stakeholders have access to accurate and current technical information, this deliverable therefore provides references to the maintained online API documentation for the relevant tools. These online resources constitute the authoritative and up-to-date source of information for API usage, including endpoint definitions, request and response formats, authentication requirements, examples, and version-specific details where applicable.

This approach ensures consistency between the implemented software and its documentation, while avoiding the duplication of rapidly evolving technical material in a static deliverable format. - Rulezet
- Kunai
- Vulnerability-Lookup
- GCVE-related components and processes

The inclusion of links to maintained API documentation supports long-term sustainability, facilitates integration by third parties, and ensures that the deliverable remains valid even as the underlying technical interfaces continue to evolve.

# Conclusion

Vulnerability-Lookup and GCVE will remain closely linked to CIRCL’s vulnerability coordination and CVD activities. Rulezet will be sustained through CIRCL’s detection, preparedness, and NIS 2-related activities. Kunai will remain relevant for endpoint visibility, incident response, threat hunting, and training.

The sustainability strategy does not rely on a single continuation mechanism. Instead, it integrates the tools into CIRCL’s broader mission and ensures that they continue to provide value beyond the formal lifetime of NGSOTI.

By maintaining these tools as open-source projects, CIRCL will continue to support transparency, reuse, collaboration, and capability building across the European cybersecurity ecosystem.
